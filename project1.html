University

Despite growing throughput rates in HEIs, many students still take longer than three years to complete their undergraduate degrees. In 2010, just 55.8 percent of public university students completed their undergraduate degrees after six years, twice the estimated duration of a three-year degree.

It is estimated that over 40% of all first-year students in South Africa do not complete their degrees

In Q2 2019, the South African labour force consisted of 16.3 million employed and 6.7 million jobless. When non-searching unemployed are included, the total number of jobless individuals reaches 10.2 million. Black Africans account for 75.1% of employment and 89.4% of unemployment. Women make up 43.7% of the employed and 48.8% of the jobless. South African youth (ages 15–34) have the highest percentage of unemployment (41.1%). The rate has increased as more young individuals have entered the job sector.

In Q2 2019, 8.2 million South African youth were not in employment, education, or training (NEET), up from 7.5 million in Q2 2013. South Africa has the highest rate of NEET individuals (15-24 years old) among BRICS countries, at 32.6 percent

The percentage for those aged 25 to 34 was significantly higher, at 48.5%. Individuals with primary or lower education had the highest NEET rate (55.3%), followed by those with insufficient secondary education (43.2%). Individuals who are low-skilled, unemployed, and not expanding their abilities pose a significant challenge for the country's formal sector

The average education level of the labour force grew between Q2 2009 and Q2 2019.

However, the average level of schooling remains low. In South Africa, 9.1 percent of working individuals possess skills that are in demand

Of those surveyed, 12.4% hold a higher education (HE) qualification, 12.4% have a technical and vocational education and training (TVET) qualification, and 32.8% have completed secondary schooling. Approximately 44.4 percent of employed individuals have not completed secondary schooling. As of Q2 2019, there were approximately 7.2 million persons.

Rising unemployment rates among HE and TVET graduates are a cause for concern. Between Q2 2013 and Q2 2019, unemployment among those with higher education degrees climbed at an annual average rate of 14.4 percent per year

Individuals with TVET qualifications experienced an average annual growth rate of 10%. The increases indicate a lack of demand for individuals with HE and TVET qualifications in the market. Individual qualifications may not be in demand, or their skills may not match corporate needs.

Individuals with higher education levels have had the fastest rate of unemployment increases, TVET, business, and engineering qualifications have reported the largest growth in unemployment.

The service sector is dominated by community, social, and personal (CSP) services, as well as financial and corporate services, which are witnessing rapid expansion. These industries require more medium- and high-skilled workers than low-skilled ones.

From Q2 2009 to Q2 2019, employment increased significantly for the following occupations: service and sales workers (769 000 jobs)

There was an increase of 644,400 jobs in various sectors, including managers (422,100 jobs in the tertiary sector), clerks (170,900 jobs in the tertiary sector), and skilled professionals (129,700 jobs in CSP services).

Individuals with incomplete secondary schooling, finished secondary schooling, certificates/diplomas, and degrees are more likely to be employed. Individuals with degrees have had the highest rate of rise.

In the secondary and tertiary sectors, there is a growing demand for degree-holders and a decrease in work opportunities for those with less education. In the primary sector, employment with degrees has decreased marginally, although those with incomplete education still make up about 75% of the workforce. There is also an increase in demand for persons with degrees.

Inadequate basic education continues to be a major challenge for the education and training system and the employment market. Only around 60% of students who enter school complete their matriculation exams 12 years later. The 2008 matric cohort had a pass percentage of 63%.

In 2017, 1.2 million students enrolled in higher education institutions (HEIs). In 2017, public institutions enrolled 53.6 percent of their students in undergraduate degrees.

Between 2011 and 2017, undergraduate enrollment increased by an average of 2.6 percent per year.

Enrollment in postgraduate degrees below master's level climbed by an average of 2.8% every year, while enrollment in master's degrees increased by 3.2% annually. PhD enrollments increased at a 9.9 percent annual rate throughout this time period.

Access to schools, HEIs, and TVET institutions has improved, but educational quality remains a challenge. Low advancement and completion rates exist across educational institutions, including schools, HEIs, and TVET colleges.

Despite growing throughput rates in HEIs, many students still take longer than three years to complete their undergraduate degrees. In 2010, just 55.8 percent of public university students completed their undergraduate degrees after six years, twice the estimated duration of a three-year degree.

The number of black African students graduating from public universities has risen. However, graduation rates vary significantly by race. Between 2011 and 2017, graduation rates increased for all population categories, but white students continued to graduate at a faster rate than other groups. Indian students' graduation rate increased the fastest over this time, with an average yearly rate of 4.8 percent.

According to the Organisation for Economic Cooperation and Development (OECD), South Africa has significant occupational shortages, particularly in higher-skilled occupations. The percentage of occupational shortages varies by occupational group, ranging from 17% for elementary jobs to more than 80% for managers. Individual skill abilities, such as reading comprehension, active listening, speaking, and writing, were found to be notably lacking in shortage occupations. Monitoring, learning strategy, critical thinking, and active learning skills are in low supply.

Many persons with higher education certificates are working in occupations that are not suitable for their skill level. In Q2 2019, just 4.7 percent of TVET-qualified individuals were employed in low-skilled occupations, indicating that the majority were in appropriate roles

However, the level of employment mismatch varies depending on the field. Approximately one in four individuals with business qualifications from TVET colleges work in non-business-related occupations

SOLUTIONS WE IMPLEMENTED!
1. Curriculum Review and Alignment:

Regularly review and update school and university curricula to ensure they align with the current and future needs of industries.

Involve industry experts in curriculum development to provide insights into the skills and knowledge required in the workforce.

2. Promote STEM Education:

One of the main roots of the skills mismatch can be traced back to high school, where many learners tend to shy away from pure mathematics when they reach the tenth grade. This avoidance is often driven by the challenges they face in the earlier grades. Even those who choose to pursue pure mathematics encounter difficulties in coping with the subject. The matriculation results of 2023 reflect this struggle, as only 63.5% of learners managed to pass pure mathematics. This trend could potentially lead to a high percentage of learners entering tertiary education to study majors that may have a significant number of unemployed graduates. Consequently, only a few will secure spots in STEM (Science, Technology, Engineering, and Mathematics), education, and health fields. Addressing these challenges at the high school level is crucial for improving outcomes at the tertiary level and aligning education with employment needs.

Encourage and promote Science, Technology, Engineering, and Mathematics (STEM) education from an early age to address the demand for skills in these fields.

Establish STEM-focused programs, extracurricular activities, and mentorship initiatives to spark interest and proficiency in these subjects.

3. Industry-Academia Collaboration:

Foster closer ties between educational institutions and industries through partnerships, internships, and collaborative research projects.

Establish advisory boards comprising industry professionals to guide educational institutions on current and future skill requirements.

4. Career Guidance and Counselling

Provide comprehensive career guidance and counselling services to students to help them make informed decisions about their education and career paths.

Highlight the demand for specific skills in growing industries to guide students in their course selection.

5. Teacher Training and Professional Development:

Address the attrition rate of qualified teachers by providing support, mentorship programs, and competitive remuneration.

Highlight the demand for specific skills in growing industries to guide students in their course selection.

6. Infrastructure and Access Improvement:

Improve overall infrastructure in schools and educational institutions, ensuring a conducive learning environment.

Address transportation challenges to reduce the number of students who have to walk long distances to access education.

7. Monitoring and Evaluation:

Establish a robust system for monitoring and evaluating educational outcomes and aligning them with workforce needs.

Use data analytics to identify trends, gaps, and areas for improvement in the education system.

8. Public-Private Partnerships:

Encourage partnerships between the public and private sectors to jointly address challenges in the education system and workforce development.

Leverage resources from both sectors to implement impactful initiatives and interventions.

9. Government Policies and Incentives:

Formulate and implement policies that incentivize educational institutions to focus on producing graduates with skills in high demand.

Provide financial incentives for students pursuing degrees in critical areas such as STEM and healthcare.

10. Community Engagement:

Involve communities in supporting educational initiatives and creating awareness about the importance of education.

Encourage parents and guardians to actively participate in their children's education and career choices.
